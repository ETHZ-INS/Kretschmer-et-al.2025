# Mature sperm harbor functional glucocorticoid receptors that impact embryonic gene expression and transmit effects of stress intergenerationally


Excessive stress influences disease risk not only within, but also across generations. Gene regulatory adaptation to stress is in part mediated by the glucocorticoid receptor (GR), one of the main stress hormone receptors. But it remains unclear, whether GR or its epigenetic signature in the transcriptionally silent male germline is directly involved in conveying stress experiences intergenerationally. Here, we demonstrate that GR does play a role in this process. Using biochemistry, omics and genetic labelling approaches, we find that GR is present in mature sperm at specific functional loci, not reflecting prior transcription, that this signature is partially retained in the early embryo, and that GR is transmitted during fertilization. A combination of genetic and protein level manipulations reveals an instructive role of GR during spermatogenesis, as well as relevance of inherited signatures and protein during early embryonic transcription. Lastly, we show in a mouse model of acute traumatic stress that circulating glucocorticoid levels, sperm chromatin accessibility at GR binding sites and female offspring behavior are altered. Our results provide a sound mechanistic basis for inheritance of the impact of stress, with putative relevance for the transmission of neuropsychiatric disease risk.
